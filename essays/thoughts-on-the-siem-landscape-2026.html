Thoughts on the SIEM Landscape - 2026

After working in the SIEM space for over three years, I wanted to share some reflections on the market, the dynamics of large vendors, and the technology shaping the future of security telemetry. These thoughts represent my personal analysis of where the industry is heading.

The Shifting Economics of Cyber Telemetry

In my view, the SIEM market is undergoing its most significant transformation since its inception. Originally built as passive repositories for compliance and long-term audits, legacy SIEM platforms are buckling under the scale of modern telemetry. I see security operations centers (SOCs) constantly struggling to process massive volumes of data from diverse cloud, IT, and remote work environments.

When analyzing this space, I look closely at the market projections from leading research firms. While they define the SIEM market boundaries slightly differently, they uniformly point to rapid expansion:

This divergence in figures highlights a trend I've been watching closely: the definition of a SIEM is expanding. Legacy standalone log platforms are losing market share to next-generation stacks that integrate extended detection and response (XDR), security orchestration (SOAR), and threat intelligence (TIP).

In my analysis of market demographics, several shifts stand out:

  • Enterprise vs. Midmarket: Large enterprises made up the majority of SIEM deployments in 2025. However, I was struck by the midmarket segment (501 to 1,000 seats) experiencing a 288% year-on-year growth rate, driven by compliance with the EU's NIS2 directive.
  • Deployment Models: On-premises systems maintained a 55.27% foothold due to legacy investments and air-gapped networks. However, the growth trajectory I observe has shifted decisively to cloud-native architectures, which are expanding at a 12.8% to 17.5% CAGR, compared to a modest 3.4% CAGR for on-premises solutions.
  • Industry Verticals: BFSI led with a 27.52% revenue share in 2025, followed by IT and Telecom at 20.9%. Healthcare is poised for the fastest growth (12.1% to 13.8% CAGR) due to mounting regulatory requirements.
  • The Decline of MSPs: Managed SIEM delivered via MSPs suffered an 88% collapse in 2025. This suggests to me that organizations are moving away from outsourced models that fail to provide the deep, tailored threat analysis required to defend complex networks.

I've seen typical migrations of this scale take 6 to 12 weeks to rewrite queries, re-architect connectors, and retrain analysts.

To mitigate these risks, I see more security leaders adopting vendor-agnostic architectures. By separating the storage layer from the analytics layer, they ensure their detection rules and playbooks remain portable.

The Ingestion Tax and Telemetry Pipelines

Traditional SIEM pricing charges by data volume. Verbose logs—such as DNS queries, firewall states, and endpoint logs—are expensive to ingest. This forces security teams to discard up to 75% of their data to stay within budget, which I believe creates major blind spots where threats can evade detection.

To resolve this, I've noticed organizations increasingly inserting telemetry pipelines (like Cribl Stream or Falcon Onum) between data sources and the SIEM. These pipelines filter noise, mask PII, and route high-value data to the SIEM, while sending raw, verbose logs directly to cheap object storage for historical hunting and retention.

We can model the cost impact of a telemetry pipeline:

S = 365 × V × (F × Rsiem - Rpipe)
Where S is the net annual financial savings, V is the raw daily ingestion volume, Rsiem is the daily equivalent licensing cost per GB of the target SIEM, F is the pipeline's filtering efficiency (percentage of noise removed), and Rpipe is the pipeline's operational rate per GB.

For example, an enterprise processing 500 GB/day on a standard SIEM ($4.93/GB equivalent, or $1,800/GB/year) spends about $900,000/year.

Adding a pipeline (operating at $0.32/GB) with 40% noise reduction changes the costs:

  • Filtered SIEM Cost: 300 GB/day × 365 days/year × $4.93/GB = $540,000/year
  • Telemetry Pipeline Cost: 500 GB/day × 365 days/year × $0.32/GB = $58,400/year
  • Net Annual Savings (S): $900,000 - ($540,000 + $58,400) = $301,600/year

This model shows a net annual savings of approximately 34%, paying back the pipeline investment within 6 to 9 weeks while ensuring the SIEM operates only on high-value data.

Architectural Paradigms: Open Security Lakehouses vs. Federated Search

In my analysis of how teams escape high ingestion costs, the industry is split between two primary architectural movements: the Open Security Lakehouse and the Federated Search Security Data Mesh.

The Open Security Lakehouse

This approach, which I've seen championed by platforms like Databricks (with "Lakewatch" in March 2026), stores raw logs in the customer's own cloud using open formats like Delta Lake and Apache Iceberg. By decoupling storage from compute, organizations pay low object storage rates for retention, spinning up serverless compute only during queries or ML jobs. In my estimation, this can reduce TCO by up to 80%.

The Federated Search Security Data Mesh

Instead of centralizing logs, federated search queries data where it lives. The central SIEM acts as a query interface rather than a storage warehouse. I see teams pushing queries directly to remote databases, SaaS platforms, and object stores (via tools like Snowflake Federation or Delta Sharing) for real-time investigation without duplicating data.

Both architectures rely on the Open Cybersecurity Schema Framework (OCSF). I've found that AI and search engines struggle with unstructured logs, and normalizing sources—like firewalls, endpoints, and identity providers—into OCSF provides the consistent structure needed for reliable correlation.

Adversary Velocity and the Talent Deficit

The most compelling driver I see for SIEM modernization is the collapsing timeline of cyber attacks, which quickly overwhelms manual operations:

  • Exploitation Windows: The mean time to exploit a new vulnerability has collapsed from 23.2 days in 2025 to just 1.6 days.
  • Adversary Breakout Times: The average time to move laterally after initial compromise has fallen to 29 minutes (with the fastest recorded at 27 seconds).
  • Analyst Fatigue: Legacy rule-based SIEMs generate over 80% false positives, leading analysts to ignore up to 62% of alerts.
  • Talent Deficit: With a global workforce gap of 4.8 million unfilled roles, I believe security teams must leverage automation to scale.

These factors explain why I see a strong shift toward platforms that automate investigation and containment at machine speed.

Emerging Features in Next-Gen SIEMs

I am particularly interested in several key capabilities that next-generation SIEMs are introducing to meet these demands:

Agentic SOC Workflows

AI agents are moving beyond simple summarization to autonomous action. I've been tracking agent swarms (e.g., Google SecOps' Gemini, CrowdStrike's Charlotte AI) that can execute multi-step workflows. On anomaly detection, they gather logs, correlate them with user history, enrich the case with threat intel, and present a structured report. In my experience, this can reduce junior analyst onboarding time by up to 70%.

Upstream In-Pipeline Detection

I am seeing next-gen SIEMs run detections directly in the ingestion pipeline (e.g., via Falcon Onum). Flagging indicators of compromise (IOCs) before they hit the database speeds up response times and avoids unnecessary database write costs.

Detection-as-Code (DaC)

I've observed a growing adoption of DaC, where security teams write and version detection logic using SQL and YAML. Managing these rules in CI/CD pipelines allows automated testing, peer reviews, and rollbacks, bringing software engineering discipline to security.

Novel Security Exploits Targeting the AI Layer

As security operations rely more on AI, I expect the AI-native layer itself to become a primary target. Integrating LLMs and CLIs into pipelines introduces new attack vectors.

For example, CVE-2026-6442 was a critical remote code execution (RCE) vulnerability in the Snowflake Cortex Code CLI (patched in April 2026). It stemmed from improper input validation of bash commands before sandbox execution (CWE-1286).

An attacker could embed malicious shell commands in a repository. When processed by the Cortex CLI, the LLM failed to sanitize the input, executing arbitrary code outside the sandbox.

To me, this highlights that because LLM outputs are non-deterministic, static scanning is not enough. I believe security teams must enforce strict input validation, run CLIs in isolated sandboxes, and monitor process spawning.

Key Trends I am Watching

Looking at the modern landscape, I see three dominant trends replacing the traditional, ingestion-taxed SIEM model:

1. Decoupling Analytics from Storage

The shift away from closed, ingest-metered data silos toward open security lakehouses (using Delta Lake or Apache Iceberg). This allows organizations to retain 100% of your telemetry at low object storage rates, keeping historical data accessible without blowing the budget.

2. Standardizing via Open Schemas (OCSF)

The adoption of OCSF normalization at the ingestion boundary. Standardizing data as it enters the pipeline provides the consistent structure that AI agents and federated engines need to correlate events and reduce false positives.

3. Using Telemetry Pipelines to Control Costs

The deployment of pipelines (like Cribl Stream or Falcon Onum) to filter noise, mask PII, and route high-value signals to the SIEM while sending verbose logs to a data lake. This reduces SIEM costs by 30% to 50%, freeing up budget for detection engineering.

Conclusion

Ultimately, my analysis of the SIEM landscape points to a future that is modular, open, and automated. The days of the monolithic, proprietary security data warehouse are coming to an end. The organizations that successfully defend their networks in 2026 and beyond will be those that prioritize data portability through open standards like OCSF and Iceberg, control their ingestion economics using intelligent pipelines, and leverage agentic AI to respond to threats at machine speed.
